The vast majority of fake or misleading news can be debunked with ease. Through careful examination and critical thinking, it becomes simple to identify fake sources or stories. Verify the source of the article One common tactic among those who publish and spread fake news is to imitate a legitimate source in the hopes that readers will not notice. A notable example of this is ABC News, whose actual URL is “abcnews.go.com”. A major imitation site at “abcnews.com.co” published a large number of articles and many people fell for the hoax. These can be difficult to spot at a glance, which is why it is important to take a deeper look before taking a story at face value. Read the whole article Some headlines may appear to be legitimate, but upon reading the article attached they turn out to be satire. They may reveal themselves by including absurd details or referencing other stories that are also false. Research the author Look into who the author of the article is. See what description the website itself gives them and look elsewhere online for more information about them. Possible signs of something being off include absurd names, unreasonable accomplishments listed, and no information about the author being available outside of the article itself. Check any sources cited Many fake stories will include cited sources that seem real at a glance. However, upon looking into them further it becomes apparent that they are not. These sources may be a government office or law, another news site, or an individual. One easy way to check the validity of the claims is to look up the source and see if they are real or support the claim being made. Check the date of the article It is a common tactic to post a legitimate article that is several months out of date if the issue discusses becomes relevant again. This is done to mislead readers and cause them to think an event occurred due to different causes or in a different context. As a result, it is important to check the date of the article being shared and see how recent it is. Look for grammatical mistakes A high number of grammatical errors could mean that the source lacks an editor, something that the vast majority of credible news sources have. It could also mean that the article is part of a disinformation campaign by somebody who does not primarily speak English. These campaigns have been growing in popularity among those who are attempting to spread feelings of division and distrust around elections.
